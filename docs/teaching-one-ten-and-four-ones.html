Executive Summary

The strongest way to teach “1 ten and 4 ones” is not as a vocabulary fact to memorize, but as part of a developmental progression. Children first learn to see parts within small numbers, then to treat ten as a complete unit, then to use that unit to understand teen numbers and restructure addition such as 8 + 6 into 8 + 2 + 4 = 10 + 4.

Across official guidance from the Education Endowment Foundation, the What Works Clearinghouse, the National Centre for Excellence in the Teaching of Mathematics, and the Learning Trajectories project, the shared pattern is developmental: build from composition and part–whole understanding, make the structure visible through purposeful representations, and require children to explain what the numbers mean, not just produce answers.

The most dependable instructional moves are consistent across sources. Children benefit when adults dedicate time to mathematics every day, integrate maths into routines and play, use manipulatives and representations with explicit links to mathematical ideas, create extended mathematical discussion, and provide brief, regular, targeted support for learners who need it.

Central recommendation Teach the concept in multiple linked forms—objects, fingers, ten-frames, number bonds, stories, equations, and oral explanation—while returning to the same core questions: What is the whole? What are the parts? How many more to make ten? Did the total change, or only the arrangement?

For the specific concepts in this report, the most rigorous sequence is: subitize and compose within 10, develop part–part–whole language, unitize 10 as one complete group, represent 11–19 as one ten and some ones, then solve addition by making ten first and explain why the total does not change when one addend is repartitioned.

Assumptions and Learning Objectives

Because the original brief did not specify an age range, this report assumes a developmental span from roughly 4 to 8 years old. That assumption is grounded in the overlap among official resources: NCETM early-years materials focus on children around 4–5, EEF addresses ages 3–7, NCETM teen-number and bridge-10 materials sit in Year 1 and Year 2, and Learning Trajectories runs through age 8.

Core learning goals

  • Represent 14 in at least three ways.
  • Say and write that 14 is 10 + 4 and also 1 ten and 4 ones.
  • Identify missing parts to make 10.
  • Solve suitable addition problems by making 10 first.
  • Justify the strategy in words, not only symbols.

Practical working assumption

A 4–8 span is reasonable for an age-unspecified brief, but the same lesson should be adjusted for a child composing within 5, a child consolidating teen numbers, or an older child expected to use bridge-10 reasoning more fluently.

What Children Need to Understand

The mathematical core has three layers. First, children need part–whole understanding: a quantity can be broken into parts and recombined without changing the total. Second, children need unitizing: ten ones can be treated as a new unit, a ten. Third, children need to use associative and commutative structure to solve calculations more efficiently.

1. Part–whole understanding

A quantity can be broken into parts and recombined without changing the total.

2. Unitizing ten

Ten ones can be treated as one complete unit: a ten.

3. Efficient structure

Children should identify addends that make ten and choose an efficient order when adding.

Concise mathematical model

14 = 10 + 4 = 1 ten + 4 ones

8 + 6
= 8 + (2 + 4)
= (8 + 2) + 4
= 10 + 4
= 14

EEF guidance emphasizes that manipulatives and representations are only powerful when children are helped to understand the link between the model and the mathematical idea it represents. NCETM similarly organizes instruction around the transition from concrete part–whole representations to explicit language and notation.

Methods Compared

No single method is sufficient on its own. Official guidance consistently supports multiple linked representations, frequent mathematical talk, and repeated work across everyday contexts. The best method at any moment is the one that makes the hidden structure most visible for that child at that point in the progression.

Best instructional order A high-quality classroom typically rotates methods, but not randomly. A sound teaching order is: objects or fingers first, then ten-frame or grouped visual, then number bond, then equation and spoken generalization.

Teaching script for representing 14

  1. Give the child 14 counters or cubes and ask them to count and build the number.
  2. Place the objects on a ten-frame, filling one frame completely before starting a second.
  3. Ask: “What do you notice?”
  4. When the child notices the full frame, say: “A full ten-frame is one ten. We have one ten and four ones.”
  5. Replace the full group of ten with a ten-rod or a bundled stick of ten.
  6. Record the equation: 14 = 10 + 4.
  7. Ask for a second proof: “Can you show the same 14 with a number bond?”
Model teacher dialogue
Teacher: Let’s build 14.
Child: I put ten here and four here.
Teacher: What do you notice about the first frame?
Child: It is full.
Teacher: Yes. A full frame is one ten. So how many tens?
Child: One ten.
Teacher: And how many ones are left?
Child: Four ones.
Teacher: Say the whole sentence with me: Fourteen is one ten and four ones.
Teacher: Can you write it another way?
Child: 14 = 10 + 4.

Helpful phrasing for adults

  • Tell me about the whole first.
  • Where is the full ten?
  • What is left over?
  • Show it another way.
  • Say the number as tens and ones.

Teaching script for making 10 first with 8 + 6

  1. Place 8 counters on a ten-frame.
  2. Put 6 counters beside it.
  3. Ask: “How many more does 8 need to make a full ten?”
  4. Help the child split 6 into 2 and 4 using a number bond.
  5. Move 2 from the group of 6 onto the ten-frame to complete the ten.
  6. Ask: “Did the total change, or did we just move some counters?”
  7. Record the sequence: 8 + 6 = 8 + 2 + 4 = 10 + 4 = 14.
  8. Ask the child to explain the move in their own words.
Model teacher dialogue
Teacher: Here are 8. Is the frame full?
Child: No.
Teacher: How many more does 8 need to make 10?
Child: Two.
Teacher: Good. Can 6 give 2 and still keep some?
Child: Yes, 6 can be 2 and 4.
Teacher: Let’s move the 2. What do we have now?
Child: Ten and four more.
Teacher: So 8 + 6 is?
Child: Fourteen.
Teacher: Why?
Child: Because 8 and 2 make 10, and 4 more makes 14.

Helpful phrasing for adults

  • How many more to make ten?
  • Split the second number.
  • Move only what you need.
  • What stayed the same?
  • Now say it as 10 and some more.

Parent script for everyday use

At home, the cleanest language is short, repetitive, and tied to real objects. A parent can use snack items, toy cars, steps, or fingers:

Parent: You have 8 grapes. How many more to make 10?
Child: Two.
Parent: Good. Here are 6 more grapes. Can you take 2 from the 6 to make 10 first?
Child: Yes.
Parent: So now you have 10 and how many left?
Child: Four.
Parent: Then 8 and 6 is 14. Ten and four more.

Classroom Activities and Printable Assets

The activities below are designed as practical instantiations of official recommendations rather than copies of any single program. Several draw directly on named NCETM ideas such as Spill the Beans, Bunny Ears, and hiding games, alongside EEF’s use of storybooks, songs, and games, and WWC’s game-based examples and maths-talk prompts.

Ten-Frame Flash and Tell

How it works: Flash a ten-frame for two seconds. Ask what children saw, then how they knew.

Variation: Use dot cards, dominoes, or a projected image.

If learners struggle: Stay within 5, then within 10, and allow a second look.

If learners move faster: Ask for two descriptions, such as “I saw 8 as 5 and 3” and “I saw 8 as 10 take away 2.”

Trade Ten Ones for One Ten

How it works: Children build 11–19 with loose cubes and trade 10 ones for one bundled ten or a base-ten rod.

Variation: Use straws and elastic bands, bead strings, or linking cubes.

If learners struggle: Work only with 10–14.

If learners move faster: Compare 14 and 41 using the same materials.

Spill the Beans Number Bonds

How it works: Spill two-colour counters or marked beans and describe the parts and whole.

Variation: Spill 8 first, then 10, then later 14 with a visible ten and four more.

If learners move faster: Record the spill on a number bond and turn it into an equation.

Bunny Ears Make Ten

How it works: Children use fingers to show 10 in different ways and discuss which hand changed and whether the whole changed.

If learners struggle: Use totals up to 5 first and allow imitation.

If learners move faster: Prompt with “Show me 8. How many more to make 10?”

Story Tent Hide-and-Seek

How it works: Hide part of a group of 10 or 14 toy animals and ask children to infer the hidden part.

Variation: Use a storybook or puppet context.

If learners move faster: Use story problems that require make-10 thinking, such as “8 are in the field and 6 more arrive.”

Make-Ten Board Race

How it works: Players move only if they can say the pair needed to make 10 first.

Variation: Use addition cards such as 8 + 6, 9 + 5, 7 + 4.

If learners move faster: Require a full explanation: “8 + 6 = 10 + 4 because I split 6 into 2 and 4.”

Animation note NCETM Numberblocks support materials explicitly describe slides that prompt discussion after episodes and note that some slides use animation to illustrate mathematical concepts.

Assessment and Rubrics

Both EEF and WWC stress that strong early mathematics assessment should build on what children already know, use a variety of contexts, and rely on careful listening to children’s responses rather than answer-checking alone. For these concepts, the best assessments are brief oral and performance tasks combined with short written transfer items.

Sample assessment items

Item type Prompt What a secure response looks like
Quick image Show a full ten-frame and 4 extra dots Child says 14 and explains “I saw 10 and 4 more.”
Build task “Use cubes to show 14. Now trade the cubes if you can.” Child exchanges 10 ones for 1 ten and leaves 4 ones
Representation match “Match 14 with the correct number bond.” Child chooses 10 and 4
Missing part “10 = 8 + __” Child says 2 without counting all if possible
Strategy task “Solve 8 + 6 by making 10 first.” Child splits 6 into 2 and 4 and explains the move
Transfer story “There are 8 shells on the beach and 6 more wash in. How many now?” Child applies make-10 or equivalent structured reasoning
Comparison task “Which is easier for make 10: 9 + 5 or 8 + 6? Why?” Child explains that 9 needs 1 and 8 needs 2
Reverse task “If 14 is 1 ten and 4 ones, what is 10 + 4?” Child connects place value and addition both ways

Open Questions and Limitations

This report assumes a broad 4–8 developmental span because the original request did not specify age. In practice, the same lesson should be adjusted substantially for a 4-year-old who is still composing within 5, a 6-year-old who is consolidating teen numbers, and an 8-year-old who should be using bridge-10 reasoning more fluently.

Official sources themselves caution that early mathematical development is not strictly linear and that specific skills may emerge in different orders. Likewise, the comparison ratings for engagement and cognitive load are analytic judgments rather than universal published scores; they are meant to support instructional decision-making, not imply that one method is always best.

High-confidence conclusion Use clear developmental progressions, daily mathematics, precise language, explicit links between models and ideas, and targeted support where needed.
