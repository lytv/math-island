# Grade 1 Math: 8 Missing Skills Deep Research

Date: 2026-06-02

This note researches eight Grade 1 math skills that would fill the main gaps in Math Island after the current 12-skill MVP. The goal is practical: explain what each skill means, how to teach it to a 5-7 year old, how a child should do it, and how to turn it into app activities.

## Executive Recommendation

The current 12 skills are enough for an MVP focused on number sense and addition/subtraction within 20. They do not cover the full Grade 1 scope. Add these eight skills as a second curriculum arc:

1. Count, read, and write numbers to 120
2. Compare two-digit numbers
3. 10 more and 10 less
4. Add within 100 with tens and ones
5. Subtract multiples of 10
6. Measure and compare length
7. Tell time to the hour and half-hour
8. Data and shapes

The last skill combines two smaller domains because Math Island already uses a 12-node structure. If you want fuller standards coverage, split it into two separate skills: "Read Data" and "Shapes, Halves, and Quarters."

## Fit Against Grade 1 Standards

Common Core describes four Grade 1 focus areas: addition/subtraction within 20, place value with tens and ones, linear measurement, and shape reasoning. The current app covers the first two partially. The eight skills below extend the app into:

- 1.NBT.A.1: count/read/write numbers to 120
- 1.NBT.B.3: compare two-digit numbers
- 1.NBT.C.4: add within 100 using place value
- 1.NBT.C.5: mentally find 10 more or 10 less
- 1.NBT.C.6: subtract multiples of 10
- 1.MD.A.1-2: compare and measure lengths
- 1.MD.B.3: tell and write time to hours and half-hours
- 1.MD.C.4: represent and interpret data
- 1.G.A.1-3: shape attributes, composing shapes, halves/fourths

## Skill 1: Count, Read, and Write Numbers to 120

### Standard

1.NBT.A.1: Count to 120, starting at any number less than 120. Read and write numerals in this range and represent a number of objects with a written numeral.

### What the Child Should Understand

A number sequence does not restart at 20. It keeps growing by ones. After 99 comes 100, then 101, 102, and so on. Written numbers show how many tens and ones are in the number.

### How to Explain It

"Counting is like walking along a path. Each step adds one. When we get to 29, the next step is 30. When we get to 99, the next step is 100. The number tells us how many things we counted."

Use a 120 chart, bundled groups of ten, and loose ones. For example, 47 is 4 tens and 7 ones. 104 is 100 and 4 more.

### How to Do It

1. Start from a given number, not always 1.
2. Say the next number by adding 1.
3. Watch for decade transitions: 39 to 40, 79 to 80, 99 to 100.
4. Match counted objects to the written numeral.

### App Activity Ideas

- Number-path tap: "Start at 86. Tap the next 5 numbers."
- Missing number: "97, 98, __, 100."
- Count grouped objects: show 11 bundles of ten and 3 ones; pick 113.
- Read numeral: show "106"; choose spoken phrase "one hundred six."

### Question Templates

- "What comes after 89?"
- "What comes before 100?"
- "Start at 115 and count forward 3. Where do you land?"
- "Which number is one hundred eight?"
- "How many: 10 tens and 6 ones?"

### Common Misconceptions

- Saying "twenty-ten" instead of 30.
- Skipping decade transitions, especially 99 to 100.
- Treating 104 as "one hundred forty."
- Counting objects correctly but choosing the wrong numeral.

### Existing Quiz Fit

Use multiple-choice and number-line. Add a 120-chart interaction later if possible.

## Skill 2: Compare Two-Digit Numbers

### Standard

1.NBT.B.3: Compare two two-digit numbers based on tens and ones, recording results with >, =, and <.

### What the Child Should Understand

Tens matter first. A number with more tens is bigger. If the tens are the same, compare the ones.

### How to Explain It

"Tens are big groups. First compare the tens towers. If one number has more tens, it is bigger. If the tens towers match, compare the little ones."

Example: 63 is bigger than 58 because 6 tens is more than 5 tens. For 63 and 67, both have 6 tens, so compare 3 ones and 7 ones.

### How to Do It

1. Split each number into tens and ones.
2. Compare tens.
3. If tens match, compare ones.
4. Choose >, <, or =.

### App Activity Ideas

- Base-ten compare: two beach baskets with tens rods and shells as ones.
- Symbol choice: "46 __ 64"
- More/less sorting: drag numbers into "greater than 50" or "less than 50."

### Question Templates

- "Which is bigger: 72 or 68?"
- "Which is smaller: 45 or 49?"
- "Choose the symbol: 38 __ 83."
- "Which number has more tens?"

### Common Misconceptions

- Comparing only the ones digit, so thinking 58 > 63 because 8 > 3.
- Reading the symbol backward.
- Thinking two-digit numbers with the same digits are equal, e.g. 27 and 72.

### Existing Quiz Fit

Use multiple-choice, drag-to-match, and number-line.

## Skill 3: 10 More and 10 Less

### Standard

1.NBT.C.5: Given a two-digit number, mentally find 10 more or 10 less without counting, and explain the reasoning.

### What the Child Should Understand

Adding 10 changes the tens digit by one more ten. Subtracting 10 changes the tens digit by one less ten. The ones usually stay the same.

### How to Explain It

"When we add 10, we add one whole ten-stick. The little ones do not change. 34 plus 10 is 44. The 4 ones stay 4; the 3 tens become 4 tens."

Use a hundred chart: 10 more is one row down; 10 less is one row up.

### How to Do It

1. Look at the tens digit.
2. For 10 more, add 1 ten.
3. For 10 less, take away 1 ten.
4. Keep the ones digit the same.

### App Activity Ideas

- Hundred-chart jump: move one row up or down.
- Tens rod animation: add/remove one rod.
- Fast mental choice: "10 more than 56?"

### Question Templates

- "What is 10 more than 43?"
- "What is 10 less than 78?"
- "Start at 62. Jump down one ten. Where are you?"
- "Which number is 10 less than 91?"

### Common Misconceptions

- Adding 1 instead of 10.
- Changing the ones digit: 43 + 10 = 44.
- Counting one by one instead of seeing the ten structure.

### Existing Quiz Fit

Use multiple-choice and number-line. A custom hundred-chart question type would be ideal later.

## Skill 4: Add Within 100 With Tens and Ones

### Standard

1.NBT.C.4: Add within 100, including two-digit plus one-digit and two-digit plus multiple of 10, using concrete models, drawings, place value strategies, and equations. Students should explain reasoning. They do not need the standard vertical algorithm yet.

### What the Child Should Understand

Add tens to tens and ones to ones. Sometimes 10 ones make a new ten.

### How to Explain It

"Numbers are built from tens and ones. To add 34 + 20, add 2 more tens: 3 tens becomes 5 tens, so 54. To add 34 + 8, add the ones. 4 ones plus 8 ones makes 12 ones. 10 of those ones become a new ten, so now we have 4 tens and 2 ones: 42."

### How to Do It

For two-digit plus multiple of 10:

1. Keep the ones digit.
2. Add the tens.
3. Read the new number.

For two-digit plus one-digit:

1. Add the ones.
2. If ones are less than 10, keep the tens.
3. If ones make 10 or more, trade 10 ones for 1 ten.
4. Combine tens and remaining ones.

### App Activity Ideas

- Base-ten build: drag tens rods and ones shells into a sum mat.
- Make a ten first: show 47 + 5 as 47 + 3 + 2 = 52.
- Choose the correct model: match equation to tens/ones picture.

### Question Templates

- "What is 36 + 20?"
- "What is 47 + 5?"
- "Add 52 + 30."
- "Which shows 28 + 6?"
- "Make a ten: 58 + 4 = 60 + __."

### Common Misconceptions

- Adding all digits as loose ones, e.g. 34 + 20 = 36.
- Not composing a new ten when ones reach 10.
- Using vertical algorithm steps mechanically without understanding tens and ones.

### Existing Quiz Fit

Use multiple-choice, drag-to-match, and number-line. A base-ten manipulative component would make this much stronger.

## Skill 5: Subtract Multiples of 10

### Standard

1.NBT.C.6: Subtract multiples of 10 in the range 10-90 from multiples of 10 in the range 10-90, with positive or zero differences, using concrete models, drawings, place value strategies, and equations.

### What the Child Should Understand

When subtracting tens from tens, count groups of ten. 80 - 30 means 8 tens minus 3 tens, which leaves 5 tens, or 50.

### How to Explain It

"A ten-stick is a bundle of 10. If we have 7 ten-sticks and take away 2 ten-sticks, we have 5 ten-sticks left. 5 ten-sticks is 50."

### How to Do It

1. Read each multiple of 10 as tens.
2. Subtract the number of tens.
3. Convert the remaining tens back to a number.

Example: 90 - 40 = 9 tens - 4 tens = 5 tens = 50.

### App Activity Ideas

- Take-away rods: remove tens rods from a mat.
- Match equation to picture.
- Number-line jumps backward by tens.

### Question Templates

- "What is 70 - 20?"
- "9 tens minus 4 tens is how many?"
- "Start at 80. Jump back 30. Where are you?"
- "Which equation matches 6 tens take away 2 tens?"

### Common Misconceptions

- Treating 70 - 20 as 7 - 2 = 5 instead of connecting result to 50.
- Subtracting only ones digits and writing 5.
- Confusing 10 less with 1 less.

### Existing Quiz Fit

Use multiple-choice, drag-to-match, and number-line.

## Skill 6: Measure and Compare Length

### Standards

1.MD.A.1: Order three objects by length and compare lengths indirectly.

1.MD.A.2: Measure length as a whole number of same-size units laid end to end with no gaps or overlaps.

### What the Child Should Understand

Length measures how long something is. To measure fairly, use equal-sized units, place them end to end, and do not leave gaps or overlaps.

### How to Explain It

"Measuring is counting same-size steps along an object. If we use cubes, every cube must touch the next cube. No spaces. No stacking. Count how many cubes fit from one end to the other."

For comparison: "If the pencil is longer than the shell row, and the crayon is shorter than the shell row, then the pencil is longer than the crayon."

### How to Do It

1. Align the object and first unit at the same start point.
2. Place equal units end to end.
3. Count the units.
4. Compare numbers or order objects from shortest to longest.

### App Activity Ideas

- Drag unit tiles along a ruler-free object.
- Pick the correct measurement from a picture.
- Order three island objects by length.
- Identify bad measurement: gap, overlap, different unit size.

### Question Templates

- "How many shells long is the bridge?"
- "Which object is longest?"
- "Put these from shortest to longest."
- "Which measurement is fair?"

### Common Misconceptions

- Leaving gaps or overlapping units.
- Using different-sized units in one measurement.
- Starting measurement away from the object edge.
- Thinking bigger units mean the object is longer.

### Existing Quiz Fit

Needs a new measurement interaction for best results. Multiple-choice can cover recognition, but drag placement would teach the concept better.

## Skill 7: Tell Time to the Hour and Half-Hour

### Standard

1.MD.B.3: Tell and write time in hours and half-hours using analog and digital clocks.

### What the Child Should Understand

The short hand tells the hour. The long hand tells minutes. At o'clock, the minute hand points to 12. At half past, the minute hand points to 6, and the hour hand is halfway between two hours.

### How to Explain It

"The little hand is the hour hand. The big hand is the minute hand. When the big hand points to 12, we say o'clock. When it points to 6, half an hour has passed, so we say half past."

Example: For 3:00, hour hand at 3 and minute hand at 12. For 3:30, minute hand at 6 and hour hand between 3 and 4.

### How to Do It

1. Look at the minute hand first.
2. If it points to 12, say "__ o'clock."
3. If it points to 6, say "half past __" or "__:30."
4. Use the hour hand to find the hour.

### App Activity Ideas

- Match analog clock to digital time.
- Move clock hands to show 7:00 or 7:30.
- Choose the correct clock for "half past 4."

### Question Templates

- "What time is shown?"
- "Which clock shows 5:30?"
- "Move the hands to 8:00."
- "Is this 2:00 or 2:30?"

### Common Misconceptions

- Reading the minute hand as the hour.
- Thinking 3:30 is 6:03 because the hands point to 3 and 6.
- Placing the hour hand exactly on 3 for 3:30 instead of between 3 and 4.

### Existing Quiz Fit

Requires a new clock question type. Multiple-choice can be used at first, but manipulating hands is better for learning.

## Skill 8: Data and Shapes

This combines two Grade 1 domains to keep the app expansion compact. For a fuller curriculum, split it into "Read Data" and "Shapes, Halves, and Quarters."

### Standards

1.MD.C.4: Organize, represent, and interpret data with up to three categories; answer total, category count, and more/less questions.

1.G.A.1: Distinguish defining and non-defining shape attributes.

1.G.A.2: Compose 2D and 3D shapes to create composite shapes.

1.G.A.3: Partition circles and rectangles into halves and fourths/quarters.

### What the Child Should Understand

Data:

- A graph organizes information.
- Each picture or mark represents one item.
- We can ask how many, total, more, or fewer.

Shapes:

- Shapes are defined by sides, corners, curves, and equal shares, not by color or size.
- Smaller shapes can combine to make larger shapes.
- Equal shares must be the same size.

### How to Explain It

For data: "A graph helps us see counts quickly. Each picture stands for one thing. Count each category, then compare."

For shapes: "A triangle is a triangle because it has 3 straight sides and 3 corners. It can be big, small, blue, or turned sideways and still be a triangle."

For halves/fourths: "Equal shares means fair shares. Two equal parts are halves. Four equal parts are fourths or quarters."

### How to Do It

Data:

1. Sort objects into up to three groups.
2. Count each group.
3. Count the total.
4. Compare categories using more/fewer.

Shapes:

1. Check defining attributes: sides, corners, curves, 2D/3D.
2. Ignore non-defining attributes: color, size, orientation.
3. Compose shapes by joining pieces.
4. Partition into equal halves or fourths.

### App Activity Ideas

- Sort island objects into categories and build a picture graph.
- Answer "How many more fish than apples?"
- Tap all triangles, including rotated and different-size triangles.
- Build a rectangle from two squares or two triangles.
- Choose the shape split into equal fourths.

### Question Templates

Data:

- "How many apples are in the graph?"
- "Which category has the most?"
- "How many more fish than shells?"
- "What is the total?"

Shapes:

- "Tap every triangle."
- "Which shape has 4 equal shares?"
- "Which two shapes make a rectangle?"
- "Is this split fair?"

### Common Misconceptions

Data:

- Counting the label instead of the pictures.
- Confusing more/fewer comparison with total count.
- Treating graph categories as ordered numbers.

Shapes:

- Believing a rotated square is not a square.
- Sorting by color instead of attributes.
- Calling unequal parts halves or fourths.
- Thinking a larger half is possible inside the same whole.

### Existing Quiz Fit

Data can use multiple-choice and drag-to-match. Shapes need a new visual shape renderer. Halves/fourths can start with multiple-choice image questions.

## Implementation Notes for Math Island

### Reuse Existing Question Types

Can use current question types:

- Multiple-choice: all eight skills
- Number-line: count to 120, compare numbers, 10 more/less, subtract tens
- Drag-to-match: compare numbers, add within 100, subtract tens, data
- Tap-to-count: count to 120 only for small/medium object groups; not practical for 120 loose items

### New Question Types Worth Adding

1. `hundred-chart`
   - Best for count to 120 and 10 more/less.
   - User taps a square or follows a jump.

2. `base-ten-builder`
   - Best for add within 100 and subtract tens.
   - User manipulates tens rods and ones.

3. `measure-length`
   - User places equal units end to end along an object.

4. `clock`
   - User reads or sets analog clocks.

5. `shape-select`
   - User taps shapes by attribute or identifies equal shares.

6. `picture-graph`
   - User counts and compares graph categories.

### Minimum Viable Content Count

For each new skill, aim for 30 questions like the current JSON pools:

- 12 direct recognition questions
- 8 model/picture questions
- 6 reasoning questions
- 4 mixed review questions

For eight skills, this adds about 240 questions. The app would grow from 370 to about 610 questions.

## Final Judgment

The current app is a strong number-sense and arithmetic practice tool. To honestly cover Grade 1 math, it needs these eight skill areas because official Grade 1 standards include numbers to 120, place-value addition/subtraction within 100, measurement, time, data, and geometry. The best next build is not just more arithmetic; it is a second island that introduces measurement, clocks, graphs, and shapes with concrete visual interactions.
