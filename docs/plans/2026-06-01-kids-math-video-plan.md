# Math Island kids video plan

Date: 2026-06-01

## 1. Deep-dive summary of the existing project

Math Island is already a complete front-end-first grade math practice app for a 5-6 year-old.

What the project already reaches:
- 12 skills across the Grade 1/Common Core path
- 4 quiz interaction types: multiple choice, tap-to-count, drag-to-match, number line
- audio feedback + spoken prompts
- stars, XP, streaks, review prompts, unlock flow, confetti
- no backend, no auth, instant play
- Animal Crossing-style island map and kid-friendly aesthetic

What the original plan was before shipping:
- Start with a frictionless personal learning app for a child learning 1st-grade math in English
- Avoid account friction and heavy gamification from existing apps
- Use visual, tactile interactions instead of worksheet-only practice
- Ship MVP first, then expand by phases

Original growth path from the implementation plan:
- Phase 1 MVP: skills 1-5 with simpler quiz types
- Phases 2-4: extend to all 12 skills and all 4 question types
- Current README status confirms Phase 1-4 are complete

Core teaching philosophy implied by the product:
- Concrete first: countable visuals, drag-and-drop, number line
- Audio support for early readers
- Small sessions: 10 questions
- Motivation without distraction
- Mastery through repetition and review

## 2. What mathematical idea is most worth explaining in a video?

Best choice for a kids explainer video:
- "Make 10 first: 8 + 5 = 8 + 2 + ?"

Why this is the strongest "hard question":
- It appears in the real content pool for add-within-20
- It is not just counting or recalling a fact; it requires strategy
- It is a bridge from concrete counting to flexible mental math
- It introduces decomposition: 5 can split into 2 and 3
- It naturally connects to place value and the importance of 10
- It can be shown visually with islands, shells, fruit, or treasure in a way kids can feel

Why it is harder than the earlier skills:
- Count to 10/20 = recognition and counting
- Add/subtract within 5/10 = mostly direct combination/removal
- "Make 10" = change the shape of the problem without changing the answer
- That transformation is the real conceptual leap

Chosen anchor question for the video:
- 8 + 5 = ?
- Then reveal the strategy form: 8 + 5 = 8 + 2 + 3 = 10 + 3 = 13

Secondary supporting idea:
- 10 is a "friendly number"
- If you can build 10 first, the rest becomes easier

## 3. Video goal

Create a short, cheerful explainer video for kids that helps them understand:
- why 8 + 5 can feel hard at first
- how to split 5 into 2 and 3
- how making 10 turns the problem into an easier one
- how this same trick works on similar problems like 9 + 6 and 7 + 8

Success condition:
After watching, a child should be able to say:
- "I can make 10 first"
- "5 can split into 2 and 3"
- "8 + 5 is 13"

## 4. Audience and constraints

Audience:
- age 5-6
- early readers
- likely stronger with pictures and spoken explanation than with symbolic notation alone

Constraints:
- short attention span
- too much text will fail
- abstract notation must come after visual intuition
- every scene should use one idea only

Design rules:
- geometry/objects before equations
- bright, warm palette matching Math Island
- voiceover in short sentences
- repeated phrasing: "Make 10 first"
- one anchor example, then two quick transfer examples

## 5. Recommended video format

Best format:
- Manim-style explainer with large simple visuals and voiceover

Target runtime:
- 75 to 105 seconds

Why this format fits:
- precise control over counting objects and motion
- easy to show splitting 5 into 2 and 3
- easy to animate a group becoming 10
- can reuse Math Island colors and island props

## 6. Narrative arc

Main emotional arc:
1. "This looks tricky"
2. "Wait, I can move some pieces"
3. "Oh! Now I made 10"
4. "That was easier than I thought"
5. "I can do this again"

Main misconception to correct:
- Kids think 8 + 5 means they must count everything from the beginning

Aha moment:
- You are allowed to break apart 5 to help 8 become 10

## 7. Scene-by-scene plan

### Scene 1: Hook - the tricky problem
Duration: 8-10s
Goal:
- Introduce 8 + 5 as a problem that looks a little big
Visuals:
- 8 shells on one side, 5 shells on the other
- Large text: 8 + 5 = ?
Voiceover:
- "Hmm... eight plus five. That feels a little tricky."
Child takeaway:
- It is okay if the problem does not feel easy immediately

### Scene 2: Count the first group
Duration: 8-10s
Goal:
- Establish that 8 is close to 10
Visuals:
- Highlight 8 shells
- Empty spaces appear to show that 8 needs 2 more to become 10
Voiceover:
- "Look at the eight. It only needs two more to make ten."
Child takeaway:
- 8 is near 10

### Scene 3: Break apart the 5
Duration: 12-15s
Goal:
- Show that 5 can split into 2 and 3
Visuals:
- The 5 shells separate into a group of 2 and a group of 3
- The group of 2 glows and moves toward the 8
Voiceover:
- "Can we borrow two from the five? Yes. Five can split into two and three."
Child takeaway:
- Numbers can break apart

### Scene 4: Make 10 first
Duration: 12-15s
Goal:
- Deliver the core strategy
Visuals:
- 8 shells + moved 2 shells become a complete row/group of 10
- The leftover 3 stays visible
- Equation appears after the visual: 8 + 5 = 8 + 2 + 3
Voiceover:
- "Now eight and two make ten. Make ten first!"
Child takeaway:
- Build 10 first

### Scene 5: Finish the answer
Duration: 10-12s
Goal:
- Resolve the problem cleanly
Visuals:
- 10 shells plus 3 shells
- Equation morphs to 10 + 3 = 13
- Final answer pops: 8 + 5 = 13
Voiceover:
- "Ten and three make thirteen. So eight plus five is thirteen."
Child takeaway:
- The answer is 13, and they know why

### Scene 6: Try it again quickly
Duration: 15-18s
Goal:
- Show transfer to similar problems
Examples:
- 9 + 6 -> make 10 -> 10 + 5 = 15
- 7 + 8 -> make 10 -> 10 + 5 = 15
Visuals:
- Fast side-by-side mini demos
Voiceover:
- "You can do it again. Nine plus six? Make ten first. Seven plus eight? Make ten first."
Child takeaway:
- This is a reusable trick, not a one-time answer

### Scene 7: Kid challenge / call to action
Duration: 8-10s
Goal:
- Invite active thinking
Visuals:
- On screen: "Your turn: 8 + 6"
- Pause beat with friendly character blinking
Voiceover:
- "Your turn. How many do you move to make ten first?"
Child takeaway:
- Practice the strategy independently

## 8. Visual language

Palette:
- warm island background
- mint/teal for active pieces
- sunny yellow for the completed 10
- coral accent for the final answer

Objects:
- shells, fruit, stars, or pebbles
- keep one object type per example to reduce cognitive load

Layout principles:
- clean rows of 10 when possible
- one focal motion per scene
- equations appear only after the object movement is understood

Typography:
- very large numerals
- few words on screen
- repeated phrase card: "Make 10 first"

## 9. Script principles for kids

Sentence style:
- short
- warm
- repetitive in a good way
- no jargon like "decomposition" or "compensation"

Allowed language:
- "split"
- "move"
- "make ten"
- "left over"
- "friendly ten"

Avoid:
- "partition"
- "regrouping"
- long explanations
- multiple rules at once

## 10. Production plan

### Phase A: Research lock
- Confirm the anchor concept is the right one for the child audience
- Use 8 + 5 as the main example
- Use 9 + 6 and 7 + 8 as transfer examples

### Phase B: Creative pre-production
- Write kid-safe voiceover script
- Decide palette and object set
- Create scene storyboard frames
- Decide final runtime target

### Phase C: Animation planning
- Build one scene per teaching beat
- Use object motion before symbol motion
- Add pauses after each reveal
- Add subtitle captions if helpful for parents

### Phase D: Production
- Animate draft at low quality first
- Review for clarity, not visual complexity
- Add voiceover
- Add gentle sound effects only if they help

### Phase E: Review with kid lens
Checklist:
- Can a 5-6 year-old understand without reading all text?
- Does each scene teach only one new thing?
- Is the "make 10" moment unmistakable?
- Is the final challenge solvable from the video alone?

## 11. Risks and mitigations

Risk:
- Too abstract too early
Mitigation:
- Start with objects, delay equations

Risk:
- Too much motion distracts
Mitigation:
- One movement per beat, limited screen clutter

Risk:
- Child memorizes only 8 + 5 = 13 without learning the strategy
Mitigation:
- Include the transfer examples 9 + 6 and 7 + 8

Risk:
- Language too advanced
Mitigation:
- Use repeated phrase: "Make 10 first"
